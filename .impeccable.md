## Design Context

### Users
Senior decision-makers and researchers at policy institutions — climate policy analysts, carbon market professionals, government analysts, and NGO advocates. Primary contexts are office research and conference presentations. They use CarbonLedger to understand the global supply-side landscape of voluntary carbon markets (credit distribution across registries, project types, geographies, and vintages) and to cite data in policy briefs, research papers, and institutional reports.

### Brand Personality
Authoritative, accessible, precise. The interface should feel like something a government analyst would trust and cite — not a startup product, not a consumer app. Confidence without flash; rigor without opacity. Like a well-produced annual report that happens to be interactive.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **References**: Bloomberg Terminal for data density and trustworthiness; The Economist for editorial clarity and confident typographic hierarchy.
- **Anti-references**: No flashy consumer dashboards. No gradients for decoration, no glowing accents, no rounded-everything card grids, no animated counters, no "hero metric" layouts.
- **Theme**: Light mode is the default and primary experience. Dark mode is togglable.
- **Color**: Functional — accent colors encode data categories (registries, project types), not decoration. Current palette (orange, blue, green, lime) is meaningful and should be preserved as data-encoding values.
- **Typography**: The current Inter usage should be replaced with something with more editorial authority. Consider pairing a confident sans-serif headline face with a refined body face that holds up at policy-paper reading sizes.
- **Density**: Information-dense layouts are appropriate and expected by this audience. Whitespace is structural (to separate sections and establish hierarchy), not decorative padding.

### Design Principles
1. **Trust through restraint** — every visual element must earn its place. Remove decoration that doesn't carry information. If removing something makes the data clearer, remove it.
2. **Editorial hierarchy** — typographic hierarchy should guide the eye from headline finding to supporting detail, the way a well-edited Economist article does. Headlines are findings, not labels.
3. **Data density without clutter** — pack in the numbers but maintain clear reading paths. Tables and charts should be scannable, not decorative.
4. **Color encodes meaning** — the four registry colors (Verra blue, GS green, ACR orange, CAR lime) are the core data palette. Use them consistently for data encoding; use neutrals for everything else.
5. **Presentable at scale** — layouts must hold up on projector screens and large conference displays, not just laptop viewports. High contrast is non-negotiable; typography must be legible at distance.
